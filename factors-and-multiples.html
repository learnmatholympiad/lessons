Big idea: a factor of a number divides it exactly with no remainder. There's even a formula to count how many factors a number has — without listing them all!

Part 1 — Factors

What are factors? 🌿

The factors of a number are all the whole numbers that divide it exactly (no remainder). This includes 1 and the number itself. Every number has at least two factors: 1 and itself.

How to find factors

Try dividing by 1, 2, 3, … If there's no remainder, it's a factor. Work in pairs: if 2 is a factor of 36, then so is 18 (because 2 × 18 = 36).

Key fact: 1 is a factor of every counting number. The number itself is always a factor too.

Part 1 — Multiples

What are multiples? 🔢

✖️

Definition

A multiple of a number is what you get when you multiply it by 1, 2, 3, 4, … It's like skip-counting. Every number has infinitely many multiples.

4️⃣

Multiples of 4

4×1 = 4

4×2 = 8

4×3 = 12

4×4 = 16 …and so on

🔗

The connection

Factors and multiples are flip sides of the same coin.

4 × 9 = 36

→ 4 and 9 are factors of 36

→ 36 is a multiple of 4 and 9

🔑 Rule: if A is a factor of B, then B is a multiple of A — and vice versa.

Part 2 — Special Numbers

Prime vs. Composite Numbers 🔬

🔵 Prime Numbers

A number greater than 1 with exactly 2 factors: 1 and itself. It cannot be divided evenly by anything else.

2 3 5 7 11 13 17 19 23

2 is the only even prime number.

🟠 Composite Numbers

A number with more than 2 factors. It can be divided evenly by numbers other than 1 and itself.

4 6 8 9 10 12 14 15 16

4 = 1×4 = 2×2 — it has 3 factors.

⚠️ Special case: The number 1 is neither prime nor composite. It has only one factor: itself.

Part 2 — Prime Factorization

The Factor Tree 🌳

A factor tree breaks a number down into its prime factors — the prime numbers that multiply together to make it.

Factor Tree of 36

36

↙         ↘

4        3

↙  ↘

2   2

36 = 2² × 3²

Factor Tree of 60

60

↙        ↘

2      30

          ↙ ↘

       2   15

              ↙ ↘

         3   5

60 = 2² × 3 × 5

💡 Tip: circle the prime numbers as you go — those are your final answers. Keep splitting composites until every branch ends in a prime.

Part 3 — The Counting Formula

Count all factors without listing them 🔢

Once you have the prime factorization, you can count the total number of factors using this formula:

If N = pa × qb × rc … then # of factors = (a+1)(b+1)(c+1)…

Example: 36

36 = 2² × 3²

(2+1) × (2+1)

= 3 × 3 = 9 factors

Check: 1,2,3,4,6,9,12,18,36 ✓

Example: 64

64 = 2⁶

(6+1)

= 7 factors

Check: 1,2,4,8,16,32,64 ✓

Example: 400

400 = 2⁴ × 5²

(4+1) × (2+1)

= 5 × 3 = 15 factors
