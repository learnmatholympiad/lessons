Square Roots √

Undoing a square — the radical symbol √ asks "what number times itself gives this?"

Big idea: squaring a number means multiplying it by itself (5 × 5 = 25). A square root runs that backwards: √25 = 5. They are opposite moves.

Part 1 — Two kinds of numbers

Rational vs. Irrational 🆚

Rational numbers

Any number you can write as a fraction (one whole number over another).

½, ¾, 7, −5, 0.25 = ¼

Their decimals either stop or repeat a pattern.

Irrational numbers

Numbers that cannot be written as a fraction — only as decimals that go on forever with no pattern.

π = 3.14159…

√2 = 1.41421…

Never stops, never repeats.

💡 Why it matters: soon we'll see that some square roots are rational (nice and tidy) and some are irrational (messy forever-decimals).

Part 2 — The Main Idea

What is a square root? √

The square root of a number is the value that, multiplied by itself, gives that number. We write it with the radical symbol √.

4
→square it (4 × 4)
16
→square root √16
4

Read it out loud

√16 = 4  → "the square root of 16 is 4."

Why? Because 4 × 4 = 16.

Squaring is the opposite

Squaring and square-rooting undo each other, just like + and −.

6² = 36    and    √36 = 6

🎯 Trick: to find √49, don't divide — ask yourself "what number times itself is 49?" → 7 × 7 = 49, so √49 = 7.

Part 2 — Tidy or Messy?

Which roots are tidy, which are messy? 🧹

Perfect square → rational ✅

If the number under the √ is a perfect square, the root is a whole number — which is rational.

√25 = 5   ✓ rational

√49 = 7   ✓ rational

√144 = 12 ✓ rational

Not a perfect square → irrational ♾️

If it is not a perfect square, the root is a decimal that goes on forever — it is irrational.

√2 = 1.4142…   irrational

√3 = 1.7320…   irrational

√10 = 3.1622… irrational

🔑 Rule: perfect square underneath → rational answer. Anything else → irrational answer.
