GCF & LCM ∩

Finding the greatest common factor and least common multiple — two tools that unlock fraction simplification, scheduling, and more

✦ What is GCF? ✦ Methods 1 & 2 ✦ Euclid's Algorithm ✦ What is LCM? ✦ The Connection
💡

Big idea: GCF asks "what's the biggest number that fits into both?" LCM asks "what's the smallest number both fit into?" They're opposite questions — and secretly connected.

Part 1 — GCF

What is the Greatest Common Factor? 🔍

🔢

Break it down word by word

Greatest = the biggest one
Common = shared by all the numbers
Factor = a number that divides in exactly
🍎

Think of it this way

You have 8 apples and 12 oranges. You want to make equal fruit bags using all the fruit — no fruit left over, same number in every bag.

What is the largest number of bags you can make?

That answer is the GCF of 8 and 12!

💡

In other words…

The GCF is the biggest number that divides evenly into all the numbers in the group — no remainders allowed!

GCF(8, 12) = 4

8 ÷ 4 = 2 ✓ and 12 ÷ 4 = 3 ✓

🎯 Remember: the GCF is never bigger than the smallest number in the group. GCF(8, 12) can't be bigger than 8!

Part 1 — Two Methods

Two ways to find the GCF 🛠️

Method 1 — Factor down from the top

Start with the largest factor of the smaller number and test each one.

GCF(36, 60)

1 Largest factor of 36 is 36 → 60÷36? Not exact ✗
2 Next: 18 → 60÷18? Not exact ✗
3 Next: 12 → 60÷12 = 5 exactly ✓
GCF = 12 ✓

Method 2 — List & Compare

List ALL factors of both numbers, then pick the greatest one they share.

GCF(18, 30)

18: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18

30: 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 10, 15, 30

Common: 1, 2, 3, 6

GCF = 6 ✓

💡 Tip: Method 1 is faster for large numbers. Method 2 is easier to understand. Both always give the same answer!

Part 1 — Advanced Method

Euclid's Algorithm — using long division ⚡

Divide the bigger number by the smaller. Take the remainder and divide again. Keep going until the remainder is 0 — the last number you divided by is the GCF.

GCF(36, 60)

Step 1: 60 ÷ 36

  1
36)60
   36
   ──
   24  ← remainder

Step 2: 36 ÷ 24

  1
24)36
   24
   ──
   12  ← remainder

Step 3: 24 ÷ 12

  2
12)24
   24
   ──
    0  ← remainder is 0, STOP!

GCF(36, 60) = 12 ✓

GCF(84, 56)

Step 1: 84 ÷ 56

  1
56)84
   56
   ──
   28  ← remainder

Step 2: 56 ÷ 28

  2
28)56
   56
   ──
    0  ← remainder is 0, STOP!

GCF(84, 56) = 28 ✓

🔑 Rule: When the remainder hits 0, the last number you divided BY is your GCF answer!

Part 2 — LCM

What is the Least Common Multiple? 🔁

🔢

Break it down word by word

Least = the smallest one
Common = shared by all the numbers
Multiple = what you get when you multiply a number by 1, 2, 3, 4…
💡

Think of it this way

Multiples of 4 are: 4, 8, 12, 16, 20…

Multiples of 6 are: 6, 12, 18, 24…

The first number they both land on is the LCM.

LCM(4, 6) = 12

🚌

Real-world example

Two blinking lights: Light A blinks every 4 seconds, Light B blinks every 6 seconds. They just blinked together.

When will they next blink at the same time?

LCM(4, 6) = 12 seconds later!

🎯 Remember: the LCM is always at least as big as the largest number in the group. LCM(4, 6) can't be smaller than 6!

Part 2 — Two Methods

Two ways to find the LCM 🛠️

Method 1 — List the Multiples

Write out the multiples of each number. The first one they share is the LCM.

LCM(4, 10)

4: 4, 8, 12, 16, 20…

10: 10, 20…

LCM(4, 10) = 20 ✓

✅ Best for smaller numbers — easy to understand!

Method 2 — Prime Factorization

Break each number into prime factors. Take the highest power of each prime, then multiply.

LCM(8, 15)

8 = 2³

15 = 3 × 5

Highest powers: 2³, 3¹, 5¹

2³ × 3 × 5 = 8 × 15

LCM(8, 15) = 120 ✓

✅ Best for bigger numbers — no long lists!

💡 Tip: Both methods always give the same answer. Use listing for small numbers, prime factorization for big ones.

Part 3 — The Secret Link

GCF × LCM = Product of the two numbers 🔗

GCF(a, b) × LCM(a, b) = a × b

Verify with 6 and 10

GCF(6,10) = 2

LCM(6,10) = 30

2 × 30 = 60

6 × 10 = 60 ✓

Use it as a shortcut

If you know the GCF, you can find the LCM without listing multiples:

GCF(9, 15) = 3

LCM = (9 × 15) ÷ 3

= 135 ÷ 3

LCM = 45 ✓

⚠️ Only for 2 numbers

This formula works when you have exactly two numbers. For three or more, use the prime factorization method instead.
