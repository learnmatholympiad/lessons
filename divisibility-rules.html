Divisibility Rules ➗

Short-cut tests that let you check if one number divides another perfectly — no long division required

✦ Rules for 2, 5, 10, 100 ✦ Rules for 3 & 9 ✦ Powers of 2 (4, 8, 16) ✦ Rule for 11 ✦ Quiz
💡

Big idea: a number is divisible by another if the quotient is a whole number and the remainder is zero. These rules let you figure that out in seconds.

The Big Idea

Why do we need shortcuts? 🤔

📚 The Long Way…

Suppose you need to know if 9,762 is divisible by 12. You could do long division — but that takes time and is easy to mess up.

9762 ÷ 12 = ??? ← tedious to compute by hand

⚡ The Shortcut Way

With divisibility rules you can answer the same question in under 10 seconds — no calculator, no long division.

✓ Is 9762 ÷ 3 exact? → digit sum: 9+7+6+2=24 ✓

✗ Is 9762 ÷ 4 exact? → last 2 digits: 62÷4 has remainder ✗

∴ 9762 ÷ 12 is NOT exact — done! 🎉

🎯 The payoff: once you know the rules, you can test big numbers instantly — useful in competition math, everyday life, and checking your own work.

Part 2 — The Digit Sum Family

Divisibility by 3 and 9 ✏️

3️⃣

Rule for 3

Add up all the digits. If the sum is divisible by 3, so is the whole number.

96 → 9+6 = 15 → 15÷3=5 ✓

So 96 is divisible by 3.

9️⃣

Rule for 9

Same idea — add up all the digits. But this time the sum must be divisible by 9.

648 → 6+4+8 = 18 → 18÷9=2 ✓

So 648 is divisible by 9.

🔗

Connection

Every number divisible by 9 is also divisible by 3 — but not the other way around.

9 rule ⊂ 3 rule

e.g. 15 → ÷3 ✓, but 1+5=6, 6÷9 ✗

💡 Why does this work? Any digit d in position p contributes d × 10ᵖ to the number. Since 10 ≡ 1 (mod 9), each digit's place value doesn't matter for divisibility by 9 — only the digit itself does.

Part 2 — Combined Rules

Divisibility by 6 — using two rules at once 🔀

2️⃣

Step 1 — Check Rule for 2

Is the number even? (Units digit is 0, 2, 4, 6, or 8?)

48 → ends in 8 ✓

3️⃣

Step 2 — Check Rule for 3

Is the digit sum divisible by 3?

48 → 4+8=12 → 12÷3=4 ✓

6️⃣

Both pass → divisible by 6!

6 = 2 × 3, and 2 and 3 share no common factors, so you can combine their rules.

48 ÷ 6 = 8 ✓

✅

396 is divisible by 6

Even? Yes (ends in 6). Digit sum: 3+9+6=18, divisible by 3. Both rules pass.

❌

21 is NOT divisible by 6

Digit sum 2+1=3 → passes rule for 3. But 21 is odd → fails rule for 2. Both must pass.

💡 Key insight: this "combine two rules" trick works whenever the divisor can be written as a product of coprime factors you already know rules for.

Part 3 — Powers of 2

Divisibility by 4, 8, and 16 💪

For higher powers of 2, only the last few digits determine divisibility.

4️⃣

Last 2 digits

If the number formed by the last 2 digits is divisible by 4, so is the whole number.

1,32 → 32÷4=8 ✓

→
8️⃣

Last 3 digits

If the number formed by the last 3 digits is divisible by 8, so is the whole number.

2,760 → 760÷8=95 ✓

→
🔢

Last 4 digits

If the number formed by the last 4 digits is divisible by 16, so is the whole number.

8,1760 → 1760÷16=110 ✓

🔍 Try it: Is 841,760 divisible by 16? Check the last 4 digits: 1760 ÷ 16 = 110 — yes, exactly! So the answer is ✅.

💡 Why? 100, 1000, 10000 are all divisible by 4, 8, and 16 respectively — so the higher digits never affect the remainder.

Part 3 — The Alternating Sum Rule

Divisibility by 11 🔀

📐

The Rule

Number the digits right to left starting at position 1. Add up the odd-position digits, then add up the even-position digits. If the absolute difference is 0 or 11, the number is divisible by 11.

🧮

Worked Example — is 2,728 divisible by 11?

2  7  2  8 ← digits

↑  ↑  ↑  ↑

pos 4  3  2  1

Odd positions (1,3): 8+2 = 10

Even positions (2,4): 7+2 = 9

|10 − 9| = 1 → not 0 or 11 ✗

✅

Example that DOES pass

715

Odd (1,3): 5+7 = 12

Even (2):    1 = 1

|12−1| = 11 ✓

715 ÷ 11 = 65 ✓

💡 Shortcut tip: for shorter numbers just alternate + and − from right to left: 715 → +5−1+7 = 11. If the result is 0 or ±11, it's divisible by 11.
